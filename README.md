# Alternative Ruthenian Immersion Pack (RIP)

The **Alternative Ruthenian Immersion Pack** is a fan-made mod for *Europa Universalis IV* that explores alternate historical developments for Ruthenia. It adds new nations, missions, disasters, decisions, and other flavour content to enrich gameplay across Eastern Europe and the Carpathians.

## Installation
1. Download or clone this repository.
2. Copy the entire mod folder and the `descriptor.mod` file into your EU4 mods directory, typically located at `Documents/Paradox Interactive/Europa Universalis IV/mod`.
3. Ensure the descriptor file sits next to the mod folder. If necessary, rename `descriptor.mod` to `Alternative Ruthenian Immersion Pack.mod`.
4. Launch the game and enable **Alternative Ruthenian Immersion Pack** from the launcher.

## Compatibility
- **Supported EU4 version:** 1.37.5
- The included `descriptor.mod` file specifies no external dependencies.

## Historical Background: Uzhhorod (14th–19th centuries)
Uzhhorod (Ungvár) emerged in the 14th century as a fortified seat of the Drugeth counts on the north-eastern edge of the Hungarian Kingdom. The town guarded the passes linking the Pannonian basin to Galicia and found its prosperity in customs duties, salt trade, and Ruthenian artisan guilds fostered under Angevin and later Hunyadi patronage. Latin Catholic elites dominated civic life, but the surrounding highlands remained overwhelmingly Orthodox and Ruthenian, preserving the rituals of the eparchy of Mukachevo even as dynastic conflicts between Angevin, Luxembourg, and Habsburg claimants washed over Upper Hungary.

Habsburg consolidation after 1526 turned the castle into a pivotal anti-Ottoman bastion. The 1640s–1720s saw Uzhhorod entangled in the Transylvanian uprisings of George Rákóczi and the Kuruc wars. The Union of Uzhhorod (1646) brought portions of the Ruthenian clergy into communion with Rome, producing the Greek Catholic church that became a cultural anchor for local elites. Habsburg counter-reformations fortified the Jesuit college and invited German and Slovak burghers, while serf communities in Máramaros and Bereg counties simmered with grievances over frontier militarisation and salt monopolies.

By the late 18th and early 19th centuries Josephinist reforms, cadastral surveys, and road construction tightened imperial oversight but also opened civic careers to educated Rusyns. Literary societies, Basilian monasteries, and cross-Carpathian trade nurtured a budding national awakening that oscillated between Ruthenian, Rusyn, and Uhro-Rusyn self-conceptions. Revolutions in 1848 briefly united local notables with the Hungarian Diet before imperial reprisals restored order. This layered legacy of border militarisation, confessional negotiation, and frontier autonomy underpins the mod’s mission tree and identity mechanics for Uzhhorod.

## Historical Background: Khust (14th–19th centuries)
Khust (Huszt) rose to prominence in the 14th century when the Hungarian crown fortified its volcanic hilltop to shield the salt road that threaded Maramureș to the Tisa valley. Angevin and Luxembourg administrators seeded German and Ruthenian burghers around the castle while Székely and Vlach settlers grazed the uplands, turning Khust into a hybrid market town that watched over levy routes and royal salt monopolies. The Orthodox bishopric of Hust maintained liturgical ties to Galicia even as Franciscan friars and royal officials carried Latin rites into the lowlands.

The Ottoman-Habsburg frontier wars of the 16th and 17th centuries transformed Khust into a forward arsenal. Habsburg captains rebuilt the fortress after the 1526 Mohács catastrophe, yet Khust repeatedly shifted hands during the Rákóczi uprisings and Kuruc rebellions, feeding both imperial and insurgent garrisons with Maramureș salt. Local Ruthenian nobles negotiated charters that protected valach law herding rights, while Greek Catholic unionists and Old Rite brotherhoods competed for parish loyalties in the valleys.

In the 18th and 19th centuries Khust sat at the intersection of imperial reforms and Rusyn awakening. Maria Theresa’s census drives and Joseph II’s toleration edicts regularised serf obligations, spawning petitions from Khust craftsmen for guild rights and burgher representation. After the 1836 salt workers’ protests and the 1848 revolutions, Khust’s fortress briefly hosted revolutionary committees that balanced Hungarian national ambitions with Rusyn cultural societies led by the likes of Aleksander Dukhnovych. By the 1867 Ausgleich era the garrison dwindled, but Khust’s schools, printing circles, and cross-border trade corridors kept the town central to Ruthenian identity—a backdrop the mod’s mission tree now translates into alternate-history possibilities.

## Historical Background: Zakarpattia (14th–19th centuries)
Zakarpattia, the far north-eastern marches of medieval Hungary, coalesced in the 14th century around castle counties such as Ung, Bereg, Ugocsa, and Máramaros. Angevin rulers cemented royal authority by empowering Drugeth, Perényi, and other magnates to police the mountain passes while inviting Ruthenian, Slovak, and German settlers to cultivate the Tisa lowlands. Salt from Solotvyno, timber from the Carpathian ridges, and customs levies on the Verecke and Uzhok passes funded a lattice of market towns where Latin, Hungarian, and Church Slavonic charters intersected. Even as Sigismund’s Hussite wars swept nearby Upper Hungary, Rusyn brotherhoods and Orthodox monasteries preserved liturgical autonomy in valleys anchored by Mukachevo and Khust.

The 16th and 17th centuries bound Zakarpattia to Habsburg-Ottoman rivalry. Fortresses at Uzhhorod, Munkács, and Khust formed a defensive arc guarding royal salt pans and Ruthenian border communities. Recurrent conflicts—starting with John Zápolya’s civil war, continuing through Transylvanian princely incursions, and climaxing in the Rákóczi uprisings—saw Rusyn peasants taxed for both imperial garrisons and rebel chanceries. The Union of Uzhhorod (1646) birthed a Greek Catholic elite that mediated between Vienna’s confessional politics and village Orthodoxy, while valach law charters upheld highland self-governance in exchange for frontier military service. Smuggling and seasonal migration over the Carpathians knitted Zakarpattia to Galicia and Moldavia despite imperial customs posts.

Habsburg consolidation after 1711 ushered in cadastral surveys, regimented border-guard units, and rulings from the Hungarian Chancellery that sought to monetise salt, timber, and pastoral dues. Maria Theresa and Joseph II’s reforms regularised serf corvée but also promoted education through Basilian schools and the Mukachevo eparchy’s seminaries, nurturing a clerical Rusyn intelligentsia. By the late 18th century, Enlightenment-inspired administrators mapped woodland commons and sponsored hemp and wine exports, while imperial road and bridge projects linked Berehove to Debrecen and Košice. The 19th century revolutions drew Zakarpattian notables into the Hungarian Diet, yet Vienna’s suppression of the 1848 uprising reaffirmed military oversight in the passes. The 1867 Ausgleich and ensuing Magyarisation campaigns squeezed Rusyn language rights, prompting cultural societies (Prosvita, Saint Basil press) to codify vernaculars and lobby for provincial autonomy. This layered experience of frontier militarisation, confessional compromise, and nascent nationalism informs the mod’s regional mechanics and late-game mission arcs.

## Historical Background: Máramaros / Maramureș (14th–19th centuries)
Máramaros (Maramureș), the mountainous county that straddled the upper Tisa and Vișeu valleys, entered the 14th century as a royal salt domain of the Hungarian crown. Angevin rulers entrusted the exploitation of Solotvyno’s halite caverns and the guarding of the Verecke Pass to castellans at Khust, Hust, and Visk, while granting valach (Vlach) law to Rusyn and Romanian pastoral communities who pledged to police the ridgelines. A lattice of monasteries—Peri, Bârsana, and Dragomirești—anchored Orthodox literacy in Slavonic, even as Franciscan friars and market privileges drew German and Hungarian burghers to Sighetu Marmației. Salt convoys floating down the Tisa paid for palisaded towns and charters that balanced crown interests with the autonomy of knezes (clan elders) and haiduk militias.

The 15th and 16th centuries embedded Máramaros in Hungary’s civil wars and the Ottoman-Habsburg struggle. John Hunyadi’s anti-Ottoman marches and Matthias Corvinus’ consolidation campaigns relied on Maramureș freemen for light infantry, while the 1526 disaster at Mohács split loyalties between Habsburg Ferdinand and the Zápolya-backed voivodes of Transylvania. Repeated raids by Moldavian princes, Ottoman pashas from Eyalet Budin, and hajduk rebels forced the crown to elevate Máramaros to a frontier county with fortified salt depots, yet valach law immunities and mountain terrain let local knezes maintain a semi-republican assembly at the “Congregația de la Sighet”. Greek Catholic union in the 1640s layered a new clerical elite over enduring Old Rite confraternities, producing a bilingual religious landscape that mediated taxation and military levies.

In the 17th and early 18th centuries the Rákóczi uprisings turned Máramaros into a rebel stronghold. Ferenc II Rákóczi armed salt miners and shepherd bands—the prototypes of the opryshky—while Habsburg generals answered with Palanok garrisons and cadastral audits to monetise salt leases. The 1711 Peace of Szatmár confirmed Habsburg rule, but Emperor Charles VI retained special privileges for the “districtus valachorum” in exchange for frontier service. Maria Theresa’s urbariums (1767) attempted to standardise dues, sparking petitions from Maramureș villages to protect alpine commons, while Joseph II’s patent of toleration legalised Greek Catholic seminaries at Blaj and Sighet, feeding a Rusyn-Romanian intelligentsia that published catechisms and almanacs in Cyrillic and Latin scripts alike.

The 19th century layered Enlightenment reforms with national awakenings. Mining schools in Sighet, forestry academies in Baia Mare, and cadastral roads over the Prislop pass linked Máramaros to Buda and Lemberg, facilitating hemp, timber, and livestock exports. The 1830s and 1840s saw opryshky and salt-workers’ revolts protesting monopolies and corvée, foreshadowing the 1848 revolutionary committees that promised equal rights to Rusyn, Romanian, and Hungarian inhabitants. Habsburg victory reasserted military oversight but conceded Greek Catholic bishoprics and teacher seminaries that nurtured figures like Aleksander Dukhnovych and Simion Bărnuțiu. After the 1867 Ausgleich, Budapest’s Magyarisation decrees pressed Magyar language schooling, yet Maramureș cultural societies, village reading rooms, and cross-border pilgrimages maintained a plural frontier identity—exactly the tension the mod reimagines through alternate missions, government reforms, and identity epilogues.
