| 6 | The Burgher estate in Ukrainian/Ruthenian lands represents urban merchants, craftsmen, and |
| 7 | townspeople organized into guilds, city councils (magistrats), and town charters. Burghers held |
| 8 | special privileges (tax exemptions, trade monopolies, self-governance in municipalities) granted |
| 9 | by princes/magnates in exchange for taxes and loyalty. However, burghers remained subordinate to |
| 10 | nobility; they could not own agricultural land (reserved for nobles), could not hold high military |
| 11 | office, and were vulnerable to noble confiscation and abuse. Urban centers (Kyiv, Lviv, Chernihiv, |
| 12 | Lutsk, Kamianets, Zamosc) became loci of competition between burgher autonomy and noble domination. |
| 15 | - XIVXV ??. Chartered Towns: Princes/magnates granted town charters (magdeburg rights, Hanseatic |
| 16 | privileges) to encourage urban settlement and commerce. Towns like Lviv, Kamianets, and Lutsk |
| 17 | received rights to elect magistrates (burgomaster, council), regulate trade, and control market |
| 18 | taxes. Craftsmen organized into guilds (stonemasons, blacksmiths, tailors, etc.) that controlled |
| 19 | production and apprenticeship. However, towns remained under noble military authority; lords |
| 20 | could levy soldiers, confiscate goods, or annul privileges. |

| 22 | Italy, and Armenia into Ukrainian towns; new trade goods (spices, cloth, metals) flowed along |
| 23 | routes from Black Sea to Baltic. Towns prospered; burghers accumulated capital, built civic |
| 24 | monuments, and gained cultural influence. Guild halls became centers of urban power; city |
| 25 | councils negotiated with magnates for expanded privileges. However, privilege remained conditional: |
| 26 | burghers could be crushed by magnate violence (e.g., during Khmelnytsky war, many Jewish and |
